Analyse the Data

Once some data has been collected, the process screen will show Control and Capability charts. In the right hand box next to each chart, smart analysis insights are displayed to help you understand your data and take valuable action.

Control Chart#

The control chart tells you whether the variation observed in the process is statistically in control or not.

  • The top chart plots the measurement data (for subgroups greater than 1 we plot the average of all the samples within the subgroup)
  • The bottom chart plots the range of the measurement data (the range is the distance between the upper and lower values observed in each subgroup)

Don't worry if this sounds complicated right now, we have put together a plain english control chart theory section to bring you up to speed. For now just remember the below points:

  • The blue lines show the measurement data that has been collected
  • The red lines show the control limits that have been calculated
    • This means that any measurements within the green shaded area of the chart show the process is in control
    • Any measurements outside of these limits would indicate that the process is out of control

Capability Chart#

The capability chart shows you whether the variation observed in the process is capable of meeting your required specification. The bell shaped curve is what we call a "Distribution Curve" and it shows how likely a measurement is to occur at any particular point, with the tip of the curve being the most frequently occuring, and the ends of the curve the least frequently occuring. So our goal is to see a narrow and tall curve that is centered around the mean of the upper and lower specification.

You can learn more about distribution curves in our theory and learning section

Our example shows that the data is distributed around the mean, and the width of the data distribution curve is contained well within the width of the specification limits, so we can say that this process is capable.

Problem Identification#

There are several different patterns that can be identified on the capability chart. This information can be used to inform actions that drive down defects and improve operational performance.

  • Process Width describes the range (or width) of the data distribution curve. In other words, how wide or narrow it is.
    • If the distribution curve is too wide, this means the process may not be capable if the width is too close the specification limits, and action should be taken to reduce the variation.
  • Process Centering describes how close the process average is to the average of the specification limits. In other words, how far is the location of the curve shifted horizontally from the center line.
    • If the distribution curve is too far from the mean, this means the process is not centered around the desired specification and may produce a biased or defect output. In this case action should be taken to recenter the process.

Between these two dimensions we can understand the variation in the process and what we need to do to bring it in line with our specification.

Important note

We should only take action to address a capability issue if the process is in control. For example, there is no point taking action to shift the process if the process is unstable, as this will give an unpredictable result.

Dashboard Indicators#

The dashboard shows the current capability and control status of every process so you can quickly identify which processes require immediate action.

This section has been a very quick introduction to the Control and Capability charts designed to give you enough knowledge to get started right away, the beauty of ControlShark is that the information is presented to you in an easy to digest way, so you can take valuable action. However, we recommend studying the further detail in our theory and learning section to get the most out of ControlShark.
