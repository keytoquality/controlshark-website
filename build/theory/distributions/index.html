Data Distribution

Data can be spread out (or distributed) in different ways. The below diagrams show some examples of how data can be distributed differently:

  • Data can be biased to the left and more spread out on the right#

  • Data can be biased to the right and more spread out on the left#

  • Data can seem completely random#

  • Data can tend to naturally center around a midpoint#

    • This is called a Normal Distribution

The red curve that is fitted to the data in this example is called a distribution curve. A distribution curve is a way that we can visualize the variation (or spread) of the data.

Normal Distribution Curve#

A Normal Distribution may also be referred to as a 'Bell Curve' or 'Gaussian Distribution'. It's a concept that describes when measurements tend to fall symmetrically around a central (or mean) value, with no particular bias left or right.

Further reading on the 'normal distribution' can be found here.

KEY POINT

In general, data should follow a normal distribution for Process Control and Process Capability analysis.

What if my data does not follow the normal distribution curve?#

If there is a concern that the data you are measuring in your process does not follow the normal distribution curve, follow these steps:

  • Take measurements in subgroups of at least 30
  • Make sure to conduct more than 30 observations
  • In most cases after 30 observations the data will be following a normal distribution curve

Further reading on the 'central limit theorem' which is the reason behind this can be found here.

Variation#

  • When the distribution curve is narrow like on the left, it indicates a smaller amount of variation because it means the measurements are grouped more closely together. There is less spread (or range) in the measurements.
  • When the distribution curve is wider like on the right, it indications a larger amount of variation because the measurements are not grouped as closely together. There is a larger spread (or range) in the measurements.
  • The height and shape of the distribution curve shows the quantity of measurements which fall at each particular point on the measurement scale (frequency of occurence).
KEY POINT

To have a tall and narrow distribution curve that is centered around the mean is our goal.
